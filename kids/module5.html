The "Before & After" Makeover
The "BAD" Slide
  • White background with black Times New Roman
  • 20+ lines of tiny text (12pt)
  • Multiple fonts and rainbow colors
  • Blurry, low-quality images
  • Word Art and distracting clipart
  • Complex animations and transitions
  • Full paragraphs copied from sources
The "GOOD" Slide
  • Dark background with white/light text
  • 3-5 bullet points (24pt+ font)
  • 1-2 consistent, modern fonts
  • One stunning, high-quality image
  • Clean, professional design
  • Simple "Appear" or "Fade" animations
  • Keywords and phrases only
Discussion Prompts: "Which slide is easier to understand?" "Which feels more professional?" "Which would you rather be presented with?"
Design Commandments Poster
1. Thou shalt use high-contrast colors (dark background/light text or vice versa)
2. Thou shalt use large, readable fonts (24pt minimum for body text)
3. Thou shalt limit text to key points only (audience listens to you, not reads slides)
4. Thou shalt use one primary image per slide (high quality, relevant)
5. Thou shalt maintain consistency (same fonts, colors, layout throughout)
6. Thou shalt use white space effectively (don't cram everything in)

Paste Special - The Formatting Savior
Problem: Copying from websites brings messy formatting, weird fonts, and broken layouts
Solution: Use Paste Special to bring only the text
Google Slides: Ctrl+Shift+V (Paste without formatting)
PowerPoint: Right-click ‚Üí Paste Options ‚Üí Keep Text Only
Alternative: Paste into Notepad first, then copy into slides
Demonstration: Show the dramatic difference between regular paste (messy) and paste special (clean). This is a game-changer for students!

The "Terrible Presentation" Demonstration
Read directly from slides (word for word)
Mumble and speak too quietly
Face the screen, not the audience
Slouch and use nervous gestures
Use excessive filler words ("um", "like", "you know")
Go significantly over/under time
Teaching Strategy: This humorous demonstration makes the "what not to do" points memorable. Students will laugh but also internalize the lessons.

5-Slide Presentation Requirements:

Slide 1: Title Slide
  • Presentation title
  • Your name
  • Clean, impactful image
  • Consistent with overall design
Slide 2: "What It Is"
  • Clear explanation of your passion
  • High-quality relevant image
  • 3-5 bullet points (keywords only)
  • Proper text formatting
Slide 3: "Why I Love It"
  • Personal connection and reasons
  • Visual element (image, icon, shape)
  • Bulleted list of key points
  • Emotional appeal
Slide 4: "A Key Fact"
  • Interesting statistic or information
  • Chart, diagram, or relevant media
  • Source citation if needed
  • Clear visual representation
Slide 5: "Thank You"
  • Clean closing slide
  • Contact information (email)
  • Simple "Questions?" prompt
  • Professional finish
Common Challenges & Solutions

Issue: Too much text on slides

Solution: Apply 6x6 rule, use keywords only, move details to speaker notes

Issue: Poor image quality

Solution: Use high-resolution images, proper sizing, avoid stretching

Issue: Inconsistent design

Solution: Use slide master/templates, establish design system early

Issue: Nervous presentation delivery

Solution: Practice multiple times, use note cards, focus on breathing

Issue: Technical problems with media

Solution: Test everything beforehand, have backup plans, download videos

Advanced Tips for Quick Learners

Speaker Notes: Use the notes section for talking points, not slides

Master Slides: Create consistent layouts using slide master feature

Embedded Fonts: Ensure fonts display correctly on different computers

Rehearsal Timings: Practice with presentation timer and rehearsal feature

Accessibility: Add alt text to images for screen readers

Export Options: Save as PDF for guaranteed formatting, PPT for editing

Module 5 Summary & Transition

Key Takeaways:

  • Effective slides use clean design with limited text and consistent formatting
  • Visual elements should support, not distract from, your message
  • Transitions and animations should be subtle and professional
  • Presentation delivery requires practice, eye contact, and confidence
  • The speaker is the main event - slides are the supporting visual aid
  • Constructive feedback helps improve both content and delivery
